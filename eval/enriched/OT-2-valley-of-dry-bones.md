# The Valley of Dry Bones: Can These Bones Live?

**EZK 37:1-37:14**

## Summary

The hand of the LORD is on Ezekiel, and the Spirit sets him down in the middle of a valley — and the valley is full of bones. Not fresh bones; dry bones. These bones have been dead so long that every trace of flesh, every membrane of sinew, every drop of moisture has evaporated. Whatever killed these people happened a long time ago, and no one buried them. They have been baking in the sun, bleaching to white, drying to dust. This is death at its most irreversible. [ENRICHED: The Hebrew word for these dead is not simply "dead" (metim) but "harugiym" — slain. Keil & Delitzsch stress that this word "does not signify the dead of all kinds, but simply those who have been slain, or have perished by the sword, by famine, or by other violent deaths." This is not a cemetery; it is an unburied battlefield. The distinction matters: these are victims of catastrophe, not people who died in their beds. This word choice is one of the strongest arguments that the vision depicts Israel specifically — a nation destroyed by violence — rather than a generic scene of death.]

[ENRICHED: The definite article on "the valley" (ha-biq'ah) is significant. Keil & Delitzsch note that it "appears to point back to Ezek 3:22," the same valley where Ezekiel received his first revelation about Jerusalem's fate. If this is the same location, the vision is set in a place already charged with prophetic memory — God is returning Ezekiel to familiar ground to show him something new.]

God asks Ezekiel a question that is either absurd or the most important question in Scripture: "Son of man, can these bones live?" Ezekiel's answer is theologically masterful: "Sovereign LORD, you alone know." He does not say yes (which would be presumptuous) or no (which would deny God's power). He places the entire question back in God's hands. [ENRICHED: The Tyndale notes sharpen the point: "The real issue was not whether the Lord was able to make these bones live, but whether it was his will to do so." Ezekiel's answer is not just diplomatic humility — it is a genuine theological question about divine intent, not just divine capacity. Jamieson-Fausset-Brown adds the cross-reference to Deut 32:39 — "I put to death and I bring to life" — as the scriptural ground on which Ezekiel's deference rests.]

God tells Ezekiel to prophesy to the bones — to preach a sermon to the dead. And Ezekiel does it. He speaks to dry bones, and there is a rattling — the bones coming together, bone to bone. Then sinew, then flesh, then skin. But they are not alive. They are corpses — reassembled but not reanimated. [ENRICHED: Clarke lays out the six-step progression with anatomical precision: (1) the bones themselves, (2) sinews/ligaments to unite the skeleton, (3) the muscular system including arterial and venous structures, (4) the dermis and epidermis enveloping everything, (5) the completed but breathless body — at this stage identical to Adam's body before God breathed into it, and (6) the entry of ruach, producing animal and intellectual life. Clarke's observation that stage 5 produces "bodies in the state that the body of Adam was before it received the animal and intellectual principle" makes the Genesis 2 parallel not just thematic but structural.] [ENRICHED: Jamieson-Fausset-Brown and Gill both suggest the "noise" (qol) at the initial rattling may correspond historically to the decree of Cyrus, which set the exiled community into motion — a political earthquake that began the reassembly.]

Then God tells Ezekiel to prophesy to the breath (ruach — the same word as "spirit" and "wind"): "Come, breath, from the four winds and breathe into these slain, that they may live." The breath enters them, and they stand up — a vast army. [ENRICHED: Keil & Delitzsch provide an important clarification on ruach here. It is neither simply "wind" nor simply "spirit" but "the breath of life, which has indeed a substratum in the wind, perceptible to the senses, but is not identical with it. The wind itself brings no life into dead bodies." What enters the corpses is "that divine breath of life which pervades all nature, giving and sustaining the life of all creatures" (cf. Psalm 104:29-30). The expression "breathe into these slain" (pachi ba-harugiym) explicitly points back to Genesis 2:7. Theodoret recognized this: "For as the body of our forefather Adam was first moulded, and then the soul was thus breathed into it; so here also both combined in fitting harmony."] [ENRICHED: The Tyndale notes add a connection the original missed: the breath filling these bodies parallels the Spirit that empowered Ezekiel himself on two earlier occasions (1:28-2:2 and 3:23-24). The same ruach that stood the prophet on his feet now stands an army on theirs.]

God explains the vision: "These bones are the whole house of Israel. They say, 'Our bones are dried up and our hope is gone; we are cut off.'" The vision is about national resurrection — the restoration of a people who have given up, who consider themselves dead. God promises to open their graves, to bring them back to the land, to put his Spirit in them. The passage is not primarily about individual resurrection after death (though later theology will extend it there); it is about the resurrection of a community that has lost the will to live. [ENRICHED: Keil & Delitzsch are emphatic on this point: the word harugiym in v.9 "indisputably proves that Ezekiel was not shown the resurrection of all the dead, but simply the raising to life of Israel, which had been swept away by a violent death." However, K&D also argue that the vision does contain a prophecy of bodily resurrection — but as background, not foreground: "the figurative meaning constitutes the foreground, and the full, literal meaning of the words the background of the prophetic vision, and the fulfilment advances from the figurative to the literal meaning." Jamieson-Fausset-Brown concurs: "Though this chapter does not directly prove the resurrection of the dead, it does so indirectly; for it takes for granted the future fact as one recognized by believing Jews." The original unit's claim is correct and well-aligned with the scholarly consensus.] [ENRICHED: The phrase "our bones are dried" carries a specific Hebrew resonance. Keil & Delitzsch note that "the bones are the seat of the vital force" (cf. Psalm 32:3), and "yabesh" (to dry up), applied to the marrow or vital sap, means their life-force is gone — not just their hope but their biological vitality as a people. The phrase "we are cut off" (nigzarnu lanu) means literally "we are cut off for ourselves" — severed from the sphere of the living (cf. Lam 3:54; Isa 53:8). This is the language of total excommunication from life itself.] [ENRICHED: Jamieson-Fausset-Brown highlights a subtle but important pattern in God's address. He calls Israel "my people" (ammi) when receiving them back into favor, but "thy people" when addressing his servants about them in displeasure (cf. Ezek 13:17; 33:2; Exod 32:7). The repetition of "my people" in vv.12-13 is therefore a signal of restored covenant relationship — God is claiming them again. As Fairbairn summarizes the logic: "He can — and because He can — He will — He must."]

## Significance

The valley of dry bones is arguably the most powerful image of hope in the entire Bible. Its genius lies in the specificity of the death it describes: not recent, not partial, but complete — dry, scattered, ancient. The vision insists that there is no condition so terminal that God cannot reverse it. It has been adopted by liberation movements, recovery communities, civil rights leaders, and anyone who has ever looked at a situation and concluded that it was beyond saving. The passage's influence on Christian resurrection theology is enormous: if God can raise a valley of dry bones, then individual resurrection is certainly within the range of divine power. [ENRICHED: Matthew Henry frames the impossibility with the Latin philosophical maxim: "a privatione ad habitum non datur regressus" — from privation to possession there is no return. That is the natural law this vision shatters. The bones are not diminished life; they are the total absence of life. And the entire point is that divine power operates precisely where natural law says return is impossible.]

## Relational Note

The relational center of this passage is between God and a despairing people. "Our bones are dried up and our hope is gone; we are cut off" — this is the self-diagnosis of a community that has given up on itself. They have internalized their death. They no longer believe they can live. And God's response is not to argue with their assessment but to act: "I will open your graves... I will put my Spirit in you, and you will live." The passage suggests that when hope is truly dead — not merely discouraged but dead — only divine action can restore it. Human effort, positive thinking, strategic planning — none of these address the problem of dry bones. Only the breath of God does. [ENRICHED: Jamieson-Fausset-Brown adds a spatial detail that deepens this: the Jews in exile "regarded the lands of their captivity and dispersion as their graves." The exile was not merely political displacement — it was experienced as burial. The promise to "open your graves" is not just metaphorical resurrection language; it speaks directly to the felt experience of being entombed alive in a foreign land. The shift from bones in the open valley (vv.1-2) to graves (v.12) is intentional: "some of the Jews were in the graves of actual captivity, others at large but dispersed. Both alike were nationally dead."]

## Conceptual Note

The passage operates on two levels of creation theology. First, the reassembly of the bones into bodies echoes the formation of Adam from the dust. Second, the breathing of life into the bodies directly parallels Genesis 2:7, where God breathes into Adam's nostrils. Ezekiel's vision is a new creation — not the creation of something from nothing but the re-creation of something from ruin. The distinction between the two prophetic acts — prophesying to the bones (which produces form) and prophesying to the breath (which produces life) — suggests that restoration requires both structural and spiritual renewal. You can reassemble the institution, but without the Spirit, it remains a corpse. [ENRICHED: Clarke's detailed anatomical mapping strengthens this creation parallel further than the original stated. He identifies the pre-breath stage as precisely analogous to Adam's body "before it received the animal and intellectual principle from God." The six-step process is not simply poetic progression but a deliberate echo of the Genesis sequence: material formation first, then the divine breath. Keil & Delitzsch confirm via Theodoret that this two-stage structure was recognized early in the interpretive tradition as a creation-recapitulation.] [ENRICHED: However, Keil & Delitzsch push back against reading the two stages as cleanly separable (e.g., stage one = political restoration, stage two = spiritual renewal). They argue against Hengstenberg's proposal that the first act represents "the restoration of the civil condition — the external restitutio in integrum" and the second "the giving of new life through the outpouring of the Spirit of God." The vision's two acts are aspects of one divine work, not a sequential program. This is a caution against over-mapping the stages onto institutional vs. spiritual renewal — the metaphor's power is in the unity of the act, not its divisibility.]

## Climate Note

The "valley" (biq'ah) in the vision may refer to the broad, flat valleys of Mesopotamia where Ezekiel lived in exile — a landscape very different from the narrow wadis of Judah. Battlefields in the ancient Near East were often left unburied, particularly when the defeated army had no surviving community to perform burial rites. The sun and wind of the Mesopotamian plain would rapidly desiccate exposed remains. The "four winds" from which the breath comes reflect ancient Near Eastern cosmology, where the four cardinal directions were associated with the totality of the world — God summons life from everywhere, from every direction. [ENRICHED: Jamieson-Fausset-Brown connect "from the four winds" to the scattering language used elsewhere in Ezekiel (5:10; 12:14; 17:21) where Israel is scattered "into all the winds." The summoning of breath from all four directions is thus a precise reversal of the scattering: life returns from everywhere Israel was dispersed. The "four winds" are not just cosmological totality but a mirror-image of the diaspora itself.] [ENRICHED: Gill preserves several rabbinic identifications of the valley. R. Jochanan (Talmud Bavli, Sanhedrin 92b) identifies it as the valley of Dura, connecting it to Daniel 3:1 and Nebuchadnezzar. Rab says these are the bones of the Ephraimites slain by the men of Gath (1 Chr 7:20). Kimchi suggests it is the same valley by the river Chebar where Ezekiel had his first visions. These traditions — though speculative — show that the rabbis understood the valley as a real place with historical resonance, not merely a literary device.]

## Modern Parallel

The valley of dry bones has been adopted as a text of hope by African American communities since the era of slavery. The spiritual "Dem Bones" (or "Dry Bones") is one of the best-known songs in American music, and its message — that God can reassemble what has been scattered and revive what has been killed — spoke directly to communities that had been systematically dismembered: families torn apart by the slave trade, cultures destroyed by forced assimilation, bodies broken by labor and violence. "Can these bones live?" was not a theoretical question; it was a survival question.

The passage also speaks powerfully to communities devastated by addiction. Recovery communities often describe their pre-recovery state in language that echoes the dry bones: "Our hope was gone. We were cut off." The twelve-step tradition's emphasis on a "higher power" resonates with Ezekiel's theology: the breath that revives the bones does not come from within them. Life is breathed in from outside. The addict in the depths of their addiction is, in Ezekiel's terms, a dry bone — not partially alive, not mostly dead, but dead dead. And the promise of the passage is that even dead dead is not beyond the reach of the breath of God.

The two-stage resurrection — first form (bones, sinew, flesh, skin), then life (breath) — maps onto the experience of institutional rebuilding. After a collapse (a bankruptcy, a scandal, a natural disaster), the first stage is structural: rebuilding the organization, rewriting the bylaws, reopening the doors. But the structure without the spirit is a corpse. The second stage — the harder stage — is reviving the mission, the culture, the animating purpose. Many institutions complete stage one and never reach stage two. They are bodies without breath. [ENRICHED: The commentary data from Keil & Delitzsch provides a caution worth noting here. They argue against cleanly separating the two prophetic acts into "structural/external" vs. "spiritual/internal" categories, since the vision presents them as aspects of one integrated divine act. The institutional rebuilding metaphor remains powerful and useful, but it should be held lightly — Ezekiel's point is not a two-step program but the totality of divine re-creation.]

## Key Questions

- What does Ezekiel's answer — 'Sovereign LORD, you alone know' — teach us about the posture of faith before impossible situations?
- Why does the vision require two prophetic acts (to the bones and to the breath) — what does this two-stage process reveal about restoration?
- How does the identification of the bones as 'the whole house of Israel' change the passage from an individual resurrection text to a communal one?
- What modern communities or situations could be described as 'valleys of dry bones' — and what would it look like for them to receive breath?
- How does this passage challenge the assumption that some situations are genuinely beyond hope?
- [ENRICHED: What does the word "harugiym" (slain) — rather than simply "dead" — tell us about the kind of death God reverses here? How does it change the vision to know these bones belong to victims of violence, not natural death?]

## Preaching Angles

- **Can these bones live?** (communities that have given up hope) — divine power that operates precisely where human possibility has been exhausted
- **Body without breath** (institutions that have structure but no spirit) — the difference between organizational form and animating purpose
- **Preaching to the dead** (pastors and leaders who feel their message reaches no one) — prophetic speech as the instrument of resurrection
- [ENRICHED: **"My people" in the grave** (the covenant name spoken over the despairing) — God's repeated insistence on calling the dead bones "my people" as the ground of their restoration, not their merit or vitality but his claim on them]
- [ENRICHED: **Slain, not merely dead** (communities destroyed by injustice, not just decline) — the harugiym distinction: God's resurrection power is aimed specifically at those destroyed by violence and catastrophe, not just those who faded away]
