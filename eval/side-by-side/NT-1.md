# The Woman at the Well (John 4:4-42) — Side-by-Side Comparison

## GROUP A: Control (No Commentary)

# The Woman at the Well

**JHN 4:1-4:42**

## Summary

This is the longest one-on-one conversation Jesus has with anyone in any Gospel. And it's with a Samaritan woman. At a well. At noon. Every detail is loaded. Jews despised Samaritans. Rabbis did not speak to women in public. Noon was not when you drew water — you went in the cool of the morning. She is coming at noon because she is avoiding the other women. Jesus asks her for a drink, which would make him ritually unclean by Pharisaic standards. He is systematically demolishing every social barrier that exists between them.

The conversation is a masterclass in pastoral engagement. Jesus does not lecture; he asks questions and makes provocative offers. "If you knew the gift of God and who it is that asks you for a drink, you would have asked him and he would have given you living water." She is intrigued but literal-minded: "Sir, you have nothing to draw with and the well is deep." He escalates: "Everyone who drinks this water will be thirsty again, but whoever drinks the water I give them will never thirst." She wants it — "Give me this water so that I won't have to keep coming here to draw water."

Then the pivot: "Go, call your husband." "I have no husband." "You are right. You have had five husbands, and the man you are living with now is not your husband." This is not condemnation — it is omniscient compassion. He knows everything about her and he is still sitting at the well, still talking, still offering. She tries to deflect into theological debate: "Our ancestors worshiped on this mountain, but you Jews claim that Jerusalem is the place." Jesus refuses the deflection and deepens: "A time is coming when you will worship the Father neither on this mountain nor in Jerusalem... true worshipers will worship in Spirit and truth."

## Significance

This passage is revolutionary on multiple fronts. Socially, Jesus crosses every boundary his culture erected between himself and this woman — gender, ethnicity, religion, moral status. Theologically, he declares that worship is being liberated from geographic and institutional constraint. Missiologically, a Samaritan woman becomes the first evangelist in John's Gospel — she goes back to her town and says, "Come, see a man who told me everything I ever did."

The woman's five husbands are sometimes allegorized (the five pagan deities worshiped in Samaria per 2 Kings 17), but the literal reading is more powerful. Here is a woman whose relational history is a series of losses — whether through death, divorce, or abandonment, we do not know. She has been used up by the social systems of her time. And Jesus offers her not a husband but living water — a source of life that does not depend on anyone else's faithfulness.

## Relational Note

The relational genius of this encounter is Jesus' refusal to let categories define the interaction. He does not relate to her as "Samaritan," "woman," "sinner," or "outsider." He relates to her as a person — thirsty, curious, wounded, intelligent, and capable of worship. His knowledge of her past is not wielded as judgment but offered as evidence that he sees her fully and remains present.

Her response to being truly known is remarkable: she does not run or hide. She runs to tell others. Being known — fully, accurately, without illusion — and not rejected is apparently so liberating that it turns a social outcast into a missionary. The text says "many Samaritans believed because of the woman's testimony." The person least likely to be believed becomes the most effective witness, because authenticity is more persuasive than credentials.

## Conceptual Note

The theological content of this conversation is among the most significant in the Gospels. Jesus' declaration that worship will transcend geographic location ("neither on this mountain nor in Jerusalem") effectively desacralizes sacred space — or rather, universalizes it. God is no longer to be found primarily in a temple or on a mountain but wherever Spirit and truth converge.

The phrase "living water" draws on rich Old Testament imagery — Jeremiah 2:13 describes God as "the spring of living water" that Israel has abandoned for "broken cisterns that cannot hold water." Jesus is claiming to be what the prophets said God was: the inexhaustible source. This is another Johannine moment where Christological claims are made through Jewish scriptural categories, accessible to those with ears to hear.

## Climate Note

Jacob's Well at Sychar (modern Nablus) sits at the eastern foot of Mount Gerizim, the Samaritan holy mountain. The well is traditionally about 100 feet deep, fed by underground springs. Unlike a cistern (which collects rainwater), a well connected to an underground water source provides "living water" — water that moves and is therefore considered pure. Jesus' offer of living water gains additional force from the geography: he is offering something better than the best well in Samaria.

The noon heat in the Jordan Valley can reach well over 100 degrees Fahrenheit. Drawing water at noon was genuinely punishing labor. The woman's choice to endure this heat rather than face the social dynamics of the morning draw tells us how painful her community situation has become. Her isolation is not merely emotional but physical — she arranges her life to avoid human contact.

## Modern Parallel

In 2021, the U.S. Surgeon General issued an advisory on the epidemic of loneliness, noting that social isolation is as dangerous to health as smoking fifteen cigarettes a day. The report found that the average American has fewer close friends than at any point in recorded history, and that the loneliest demographics are young adults (ages 15-24) and people who have experienced repeated relational loss.

The woman at the well is the patron saint of the lonely. Her five marriages are not a sign of promiscuity but of repeated loss — in a culture where women could not initiate divorce, she has likely been discarded five times. She draws water alone in the heat of the day because human community has become more painful than physical discomfort. And into that isolation steps someone who knows everything about her and wants to talk anyway.

The modern parallel is not just to loneliness but to the way we manage our shame. We all have "noon strategies" — behaviors designed to avoid being seen, known, or exposed. We curate social media profiles, choose surface-level friendships, and deflect personal questions into abstract debates (exactly as the woman does: "Let's talk about theology instead of my life"). Jesus refuses to be deflected. He insists on both — the personal truth AND the theological truth — because for him, they are inseparable.

## Key Questions

- Why does Jesus choose to have his longest recorded conversation with someone from the most despised social group, and what does this tell us about divine priorities?
- How does Jesus navigate the woman's deflection from personal to theological topics, and what can we learn from his approach?
- What does 'worship in Spirit and truth' mean practically — how is it different from worship tied to a specific location or tradition?
- The woman tells her town, 'Come see a man who told me everything I ever did' — why is being fully known the thing she leads with?
- How does this passage challenge the assumption that effective witnesses must have perfect backgrounds?

## Preaching Angles

- **The Noon Strategy: How We Avoid Being Known** (people carrying shame) — vulnerability and grace
- **Five Husbands and Living Water: When Human Love Isn't Enough** (those with relational wounds) — divine sufficiency
- **The Unlikely Evangelist: Why Broken People Are the Best Witnesses** (those who feel disqualified from ministry) — calling and authenticity

[ENRICHED: The depth of the Jewish-Samaritan enmity deserves expansion. According to rabbinic tradition (cited by Matthew Henry via Rabbi Tanchum), Jews "looked upon [Samaritans] as having no part in the resurrection, excommunicated and cursed them by the sacred name of God, by the glorious writing of the tables, and by the curse of the upper and lower house of judgment, with this law, That no Israelite eat of any thing that is a Samaritan's, for it is as if he should eat swine's flesh." The Greek word for "dealings" (sunchrontai) means more than commercial transactions — Adam Clarke notes it implies "communion" in the fullest sense: borrowing nothing, not drinking from the same vessel, having no religious connection. The woman likely identified Jesus as Jewish by his Galilean dialect (cf. Mark 14:70) and possibly his dress (Clarke). Her question is not just surprise — JFB reads it as "not altogether refusing, yet wondering at so unusual a request." She is navigating the politics of mutual contempt while something in this stranger's manner holds her attention.]

The conversation is a masterclass in pastoral engagement. Jesus does not lecture; he asks questions and makes provocative offers. "If you knew the gift of God and who it is that asks you for a drink, you would have asked him and he would have given you living water." She is intrigued but literal-minded: "Sir, you have nothing to draw with and the well is deep." He escalates: "Everyone who drinks this water will be thirsty again, but whoever drinks the water I give them will never thirst." She wants it — "Give me this water so that I won't have to keep coming here to draw water."

[ENRICHED: The commentaries sharpen the dynamic of this exchange. JFB observes that Jesus' opening move is a deliberate role-reversal: "In Me thou seest only a petitioner to thee but if thou knewest who that Petitioner is, and the Gift that God is giving to men, thou wouldst have changed places with Him." Christ puts himself in the position of need — the thirsty one — so that she can discover she is the one who truly needs something from him. John Gill reads "the gift of God" as referring to Christ himself (cf. Isaiah 9:6, John 3:16) — not just something God gives, but the person standing before her. On the woman's response in v.15, the commentaries disagree. Gill reads her as still "sneering and scoffing." JFB disagrees: "This is not obtuseness — that is giving way — it expresses a wondering desire after she scarce knew what from this mysterious Stranger." Matthew Henry offers both readings and leaves it open. The ambiguity is itself revealing — she is somewhere between mockery and longing, and Jesus meets her there.]

Then the pivot: "Go, call your husband." "I have no husband." "You are right. You have had five husbands, and the man you are living with now is not your husband." This is not condemnation — it is omniscient compassion. He knows everything about her and he is still sitting at the well, still talking, still offering. She tries to deflect into theological debate: "Our ancestors worshiped on this mountain, but you Jews claim that Jerusalem is the place." Jesus refuses the deflection and deepens: "A time is coming when you will worship the Father neither on this mountain nor in Jerusalem... true worshipers will worship in Spirit and truth."

[ENRICHED: The commentary tradition is divided on how to read the "five husbands" detail, and this division is worth noting. Adam Clarke's commentary (citing Bishop Pearce) mounts a sustained argument that the woman may not have been an adulteress at all: (1) a woman advanced enough in years to have had five husbands is unlikely to be found in active adultery; (2) Jesus never directly reproves her for fornication anywhere in the text; (3) a woman of genuinely bad reputation would be unlikely to have such influence that her townspeople would believe her testimony (v.39-42); (4) a guilty woman confronted by a prophet would not immediately pivot to high theology; (5) it is improbable that Jesus would make his clearest self-disclosure as Messiah to an unrepentant sinner. Clarke suggests the man she is currently with may be a betrothed partner not yet brought home. This is a minority reading, but it checks the common assumption that she was promiscuous. Gill, by contrast, reads the five husbands as likely divorced for adultery. Matthew Henry reads them as a sign of her sin — "either she had eloped, or by her undutiful, unclean, disloyal conduct, had provoked them to divorce her." The text itself is genuinely ambiguous, and that ambiguity may be deliberate.

On the theological "deflection," JFB offers a remarkably generous reading of Jesus' pastoral method: "The Prince of preachers takes another method: He humors the poor woman, letting her take her own way, allowing her to lead while He follows — but thus only the more effectually gaining His object." Jesus does not say, "That question is not the point right now." He answers her question, pours light into her mind, and brings her "insensibly to the point at which He could disclose to her wondering mind whom she was all the while speaking to." This is not deflection being shut down — it is deflection being honored and then surpassed.]

[ENRICHED: The commentaries add a striking detail to the missiological significance. JFB and Clarke both note that Jesus "scarce ever said anything like this to His own people, the Jews" — his direct declaration "I am he" (the Messiah) in v.26 is more explicit than virtually anything he says to Jewish audiences or even his own disciples until near the end of his ministry. Clarke explains this as a strategic calculation: disclosure to a Samaritan woman would not jeopardize his ministry the way a public messianic claim among Jews would. But there is something more radical at work — the person least expected to receive this revelation is the first to receive it. Additionally, Tyndale notes that the Greek of v.26 ("I am, the one speaking to you") carries overtones of the divine name from Exodus 3:14, making this not just a messianic claim but a theophanic one.]

Here is a woman whose relational history is a series of losses — whether through death, divorce, or abandonment, we do not know. She has been used up by the social systems of her time. And Jesus offers her not a husband but living water — a source of life that does not depend on anyone else's faithfulness.

[ENRICHED: The allegorical reading has more historical grounding than the original notes convey, though the conclusion that the literal reading is stronger still holds. Gill and Clarke both document that the Samaritans were a mixed population — descended not from the original ten tribes but from colonists sent by the king of Assyria from "Babylon, Cuthah, Ava, Hamath, and Sepharvaim" (2 Kings 17:24), each group bringing its own gods. Gill's extensive citations from rabbinic sources record that the Samaritans were accused of worshiping fire, a dove-image on Gerizim, and the teraphim Jacob buried under the oak at Shechem (Gen 35:4). So the five-husbands-as-five-deities reading has real historical texture. But the commentators themselves mostly resist it: Clarke, Gill, and Henry all treat the five husbands as literal husbands, and the power of the passage depends on Jesus knowing the actual details of this particular woman's life — not reciting national-religious allegory.]

He relates to her as a person — thirsty, curious, wounded, intelligent, and capable of worship. His knowledge of her past is not wielded as judgment but offered as evidence that he sees her fully and remains present.

[ENRICHED: Matthew Henry makes a sharp observation about Jesus' rhetorical technique when revealing her past. When the woman says "I have no husband," Jesus does not call her a strumpet or condemn her directly. Instead, "he puts a better construction than it would well bear upon what she said by way of shuffle and evasion" — turning her evasion into a confession by saying "You have well said, I have no husband." Henry draws from this the principle: "Reproofs are ordinarily most profitable when they are least provoking." And: "Those who would win souls should make the best of them, whereby they may hope to work upon their good-nature; for, if they make the worst of them, they certainly exasperate their ill-nature." This is the pastoral genius the original correctly identifies, now grounded in specific textual mechanics.]

[ENRICHED: Tyndale notes a significant narrative arc in how the woman names Jesus across the conversation — she moves from "a Jew" (v.9) to "Sir" (v.11, 15, 19) to "a prophet" (v.19) to a possible Messiah (v.29) — and finally the townspeople arrive at "Savior of the world" (v.42). This progression of recognition is itself a miniature discipleship journey, compressed into one conversation. JFB also observes that by the end of the dialogue, the woman's "simplicity" has "become perfect" — she has been brought "up from her sunken condition to a frame of mind and heart capable of the noblest revelations." The witness she becomes is not separate from the encounter — it is its natural overflow.]

## Conceptual Note

The theological content of this conversation is among the most significant in the Gospels. Jesus' declaration that worship will transcend geographic location ("neither on this mountain nor in Jerusalem") effectively desacralizes sacred space — or rather, universalizes it. God is no longer to be found primarily in a temple or on a mountain but wherever Spirit and truth converge.

[ENRICHED: The commentaries fill in the historical stakes of the Gerizim-vs-Jerusalem dispute that the original merely sketches. The Samaritan temple on Mount Gerizim was built by Sanballat for his son-in-law Manasseh (a Jewish priest expelled for marrying Sanballat's daughter), around 332 BC, with permission from Alexander the Great (Josephus, Antiquities 11.8.4). It stood for roughly 200 years before being destroyed by John Hyrcanus. Gill and Clarke both note that the Samaritan Pentateuch reads "Gerizim" where the Hebrew Pentateuch reads "Ebal" in Deuteronomy 27:4 — a textual alteration meant to legitimate their worship site (Clarke notes Kennicott argued Gerizim was the original reading, though Jesus' answer renders the question moot). JFB unpacks Jesus' response as containing three distinct claims: (1) the dispute will soon become irrelevant because of a total change of dispensation; (2) the Samaritans are wrong not just about the place but about the nature and grounds of their worship, while truth lies with the Jews; (3) God as Spirit invites and demands spiritual worship, superseding worship by "consecrated persons, place, and times." Jesus' word "Father" is itself significant — JFB notes that "she had talked simply of 'worship'; our Lord brings up before her the great OBJECT of all acceptable worship — 'THE FATHER'" — shifting from where to whom.]

[ENRICHED: On "worship in spirit and truth" — Tyndale notes that a single Greek preposition governs both words (literally "in spirit and truth"), making them a unified concept rather than two separate requirements. Clarke expands: "A man worships God in spirit, when, under the influence of the Holy Ghost, he brings all his affections, appetites, and desires to the throne of God; and he worships him in truth, when every purpose and passion of his heart, and when every act of his religious worship, is guided and regulated by the word of God." This is both more inward and more demanding than simply "worship without a building."]

The phrase "living water" draws on rich Old Testament imagery — Jeremiah 2:13 describes God as "the spring of living water" that Israel has abandoned for "broken cisterns that cannot hold water." Jesus is claiming to be what the prophets said God was: the inexhaustible source. This is another Johannine moment where Christological claims are made through Jewish scriptural categories, accessible to those with ears to hear.

[ENRICHED: The commentaries add technical precision to the "living water" metaphor. Clarke notes that "living water" in both Eastern and Western usage "always meant spring water, in opposition to dead, stagnant water contained in ponds, pools, tanks, or cisterns." JFB draws a crucial distinction between two Greek words the English translation obscures: the word rendered "well" in v.11 (describing Jacob's well) is different from the word better rendered "fountain" in v.14 (describing what Christ gives). The soul receiving Christ's water becomes "not a cistern, for holding water poured into it from without, but a fountain, springing, gushing, bubbling up and flowing forth within us, ever fresh, ever living." The contrast is between an external supply (always running out, always needing refilling) and an internal spring (self-renewing, inexhaustible). Gill further documents the Jewish linguistic convention: in Hebrew, "living water" (mayim chayyim, cf. Gen 26:19) specifically denotes water that is "always flowing, and never ceases."]

[ENRICHED: The commentaries provide richer geographic and archaeological detail. Clarke cites the travel account of Maundrell, who describes Jacob's well as "dug in a firm rock, about three yards in diameter, and thirty-five in depth, five of which [they] found full of water." An old stone vault covers it, and a large church built by the Empress Irene once stood over it (now in ruins). Maundrell notes that the well sits where "the narrow valley of Sychem ends, opening itself into a wide field, which probably is part of the ground given by Jacob to his son Joseph. It is watered by a fresh stream, running between it and Shechem, which makes it exceedingly verdant and fruitful." The city of Sychar itself was about thirty-four miles from Jerusalem (JFB). Clarke also notes the likely etymology: "Sychar" may derive from a word meaning "drunken," referencing Isaiah's charge against the Ephraimites (Isa 28:1, 3, 7, 8) — a connection Gill confirms. The original's "about 100 feet deep" should be noted as approximate — Clarke records thirty-five yards (105 feet), while Gill cites another tradition of forty cubits (roughly sixty feet).]

The woman's choice to endure this heat rather than face the social dynamics of the morning draw tells us how painful her community situation has become. [ENRICHED: JFB adds a vivid physical detail about the scene: Christ's weariness is described as "perhaps the most human of all the scenes of our Lord's earthly history." The word "thus" (houtos) in "sat thus on the well" means, as Gill paraphrases, "he sat like a weary person, glad to set himself down any where; and not caring how, or where, he sat to rest his weary limbs" — not on a throne or cushion, but on the bare ground or stone rim. Matthew Henry observes that Christ's disciples could walk into town without difficulty while he could not go "a step further," suggesting that "bodies of the finest mould are most sensible of fatigue." The sixth hour (noon) accounts for Christ's fatigue from traveling all morning, his thirst from the midday heat, and the disciples' errand to buy food at the ordinary Jewish dinner hour (Clarke). Into this scene of genuine human exhaustion walks a woman whose own exhaustion is social rather than physical.]

## GROUP C: Regeneration (From Scratch with Commentary)

# The Woman at the Well

**JHN 4:4-42**

## Summary

Jesus is traveling from Judea to Galilee, and the text says he "had to pass through Samaria" (v.4). On one level this is simple geography -- Samaria sat between the two regions, and Josephus confirms the route took about three days (Clarke). But John Gill sees a second necessity at work: "the calling and conversion of a certain woman, and other Samaritans, whom the Father had given to him." Geographic necessity and divine appointment converge at a well outside the town of Sychar, the ancient Shechem, on ground Jacob had given to his son Joseph (Gen 33:19; 48:22).

It is about noon. Jesus is exhausted. The word "thus" in "sat thus on the well" is vivid -- Gill paraphrases it as "he sat like a weary person, glad to set himself down any where; and not caring how, or where, he sat to rest his weary limbs." Jamieson-Fausset-Brown calls this "perhaps the most human of all the scenes of our Lord's earthly history." He is sitting on the bare stone rim of a well dug in solid rock, roughly three yards across and thirty-five yards deep (Clarke, citing the traveler Maundrell). His disciples have walked into town to buy food, leaving him alone.

Into this scene walks a woman drawing water at the worst possible hour. Women drew water in early morning or evening, when the air was cool and the well was a social gathering point. Coming at noon meant enduring punishing heat -- but it also meant avoiding every other woman in town. Whatever her story is, she has arranged her daily life around not being seen.

Jesus opens with a request: "Give me a drink." This is a small sentence carrying enormous social weight. The Jewish-Samaritan hostility was centuries deep. Clarke explains that the Greek word for "dealings" (sunchrontai) implies full "communion" -- Jews would not borrow from Samaritans, would not drink from the same vessel, would not share a meal or engage in any religious connection. Matthew Henry, citing Rabbi Tanchum, records that Jews "looked upon them as having no part in the resurrection, excommunicated and cursed them by the sacred name of God... with this law, That no Israelite eat of any thing that is a Samaritan's, for it is as if he should eat swine's flesh." Add to this the rabbinic prohibition against a man speaking to a woman in public, and what Jesus does by asking for water is a quiet act of social demolition.

The woman notices. "You are a Jew -- how can you ask me for a drink?" She likely identified him by his Galilean dialect (Clarke, cf. Mark 14:70) and possibly his dress. JFB reads her tone as "not altogether refusing, yet wondering at so unusual a request." She is navigating mutual contempt while something about this stranger holds her attention.

Jesus pivots from the literal to the spiritual: "If you knew the gift of God and who is asking you for a drink, you would have asked him, and he would have given you living water." JFB captures the role-reversal: "In Me thou seest only a petitioner to thee but if thou knewest who that Petitioner is... thou wouldst have changed places with Him, gladly suing of Him living water." The divine one puts himself in the position of need so that the truly needy one can discover what she actually lacks. Gill reads "the gift of God" as Christ himself (cf. Isa 9:6; John 3:16) -- not merely a spiritual blessing but the person standing before her.

She takes him literally: "Sir, you have nothing to draw with and the well is deep." Clarke notes that travelers needed their own leather buckets and ropes for deep wells -- Jesus apparently had neither. Her question "Are you greater than our father Jacob?" contains a buried irony: the Samaritans claimed Jacob as ancestor, though they were in fact descended from Assyrian colonists resettled after the ten tribes were deported (2 Kings 17:24; Gill, Clarke).

Jesus does not answer the irony. He answers the thirst: "Everyone who drinks this water will be thirsty again. But whoever drinks the water I give him will never thirst. Indeed, the water I give him will become in him a fount of water springing up to eternal life." JFB draws a crucial distinction hidden in the Greek: the word for "well" in v.11 (Jacob's well) is different from the word better translated "fountain" in v.14 (what Christ gives). The soul receiving this water becomes "not a cistern, for holding water poured into it from without, but a fountain, springing, gushing, bubbling up and flowing forth within us, ever fresh, ever living." Clarke adds that "living water" in both Eastern and Western usage always meant spring water -- flowing, moving, pure -- as opposed to "dead, stagnant water contained in ponds, pools, tanks, or cisterns." In Hebrew, mayim chayyim denotes water "always flowing, and never ceasing" (Gill, cf. Gen 26:19).

The woman's response in v.15 -- "Sir, give me this water so that I will not get thirsty and have to keep coming here" -- divides the commentators. Gill reads her as still mocking. JFB disagrees entirely: "This is not obtuseness -- that is giving way -- it expresses a wondering desire after she scarce knew what from this mysterious Stranger." Henry offers both readings and leaves the question open. The ambiguity itself is telling. She is somewhere between scorn and longing, and Jesus meets her exactly there.

Then the conversation turns. "Go, call your husband and come back." "I have no husband." Jesus replies: "You are correct. You have had five husbands, and the man you now have is not your husband. You have spoken truthfully." The commentary tradition is genuinely divided on what this reveals about her. Clarke, citing Bishop Pearce, mounts a five-point argument that she may not have been immoral at all: (1) a woman old enough to have had five husbands is unlikely to be found in active adultery; (2) Jesus never directly reproves her for sexual sin anywhere in the text; (3) a woman of truly bad reputation would not have had the social standing to persuade her townspeople (vv.39-42); (4) someone caught in guilt by a prophet would not immediately pivot to high theology; (5) Jesus' most explicit messianic self-disclosure (v.26) to an unrepentant sinner would be without parallel. Clarke suggests the current man may be a betrothed partner not yet formally married. Gill, by contrast, reads the five husbands as likely divorced for adultery. Henry reads them as evidence of sin -- "either she had eloped, or by her undutiful, unclean, disloyal conduct, had provoked them to divorce her." The text itself declines to specify, and that reticence may be deliberate.

What is not ambiguous is Jesus' method. Henry makes a sharp observation: Jesus does not call her a "strumpet." When she says "I have no husband," he turns her evasion into a confession by saying "You have well said, I have no husband" -- "he puts a better construction than it would well bear upon what she said by way of shuffle and evasion." Henry draws the principle: "Reproofs are ordinarily most profitable when they are least provoking."

The woman shifts ground: "I see that you are a prophet. Our fathers worshiped on this mountain, but you Jews say Jerusalem is the place." JFB offers a remarkably generous reading of this apparent deflection: "The Prince of preachers takes another method: He humors the poor woman, letting her take her own way, allowing her to lead while He follows -- but thus only the more effectually gaining His object." Jesus does not say, "We are not discussing theology right now." He answers her question, pours light into her mind about the nature of true worship, and brings her "insensibly to the point at which He could disclose to her wondering mind whom she was all the while speaking to."

Jesus' answer contains three revolutionary claims (JFB's analysis): (1) The Gerizim-versus-Jerusalem dispute will soon be irrelevant because a total change of dispensation is coming. (2) The Samaritans are wrong not merely about the location but about the grounds and nature of their worship, while the truth in these matters lies with the Jews -- "salvation is from the Jews." (3) God as Spirit invites and demands spiritual worship, superseding worship tied to consecrated persons, places, and times. His pivot from "worship" (her word) to "the Father" (his word) is itself significant -- JFB notes he shifts the question from where to whom.

On "worship in spirit and truth," Tyndale observes that a single Greek preposition governs both words, making them one unified concept rather than two parallel requirements. Clarke expands: "A man worships God in spirit, when, under the influence of the Holy Ghost, he brings all his affections, appetites, and desires to the throne of God; and he worships him in truth, when every purpose and passion of his heart, and when every act of his religious worship, is guided and regulated by the word of God." This is both more inward and more demanding than the modern shorthand of "worship without a building."

The woman says, "I know that Messiah is coming. When he comes, he will explain everything to us." Jesus replies: "I who speak to you am he." Clarke, JFB, and Gill all note the extraordinary character of this moment: Jesus "scarce ever said anything like this to His own people, the Jews" (JFB). This is more direct than virtually anything he says to Jewish audiences or his own disciples until the final week. Clarke explains the strategy -- disclosure to a Samaritan woman would not threaten his ministry the way a public Jewish claim would -- but there is something beyond strategy here. Tyndale notes that the Greek phrasing ("I am, the one speaking to you") carries overtones of the divine name from Exodus 3:14. This is not only a messianic claim but a theophanic one.

The disciples return and are visibly startled, but ask nothing. The woman abandons her water jar -- a detail that speaks volumes about her internal state -- and runs into town with words that are half testimony, half question: "Come, see a man who told me everything I ever did. Could this be the Christ?" The townspeople come. Jesus stays two days. "Many more believed because of his message." And the final verdict belongs to the Samaritans themselves: "We now believe not only because of your words; we have heard for ourselves, and we know that this man truly is the Savior of the world."

## Significance

This passage operates on every register at once -- social, theological, missiological, and deeply personal.

Socially, Jesus systematically crosses every boundary his culture had erected: ethnicity (Jew to Samaritan), gender (rabbi to woman in public), religion (orthodox to heterodox), and moral status (righteous to suspect). He does not merely tolerate her; he initiates the conversation. He puts himself in the position of needing something from her. Clarke, Henry, and JFB all register the shock of this: a Jewish man asking a Samaritan woman to share a drinking vessel would have been unthinkable.

Theologically, the passage delivers some of the most concentrated Christological and worship theology in the Gospels. Jesus' declaration that worship will transcend geographic location ("neither on this mountain nor in Jerusalem") effectively universalizes sacred space. His claim to offer "living water" draws on Jeremiah 2:13, where God describes himself as "the spring of living water" that Israel has abandoned for "broken cisterns that cannot hold water." Jesus is claiming to be what the prophets said God was: the inexhaustible source. JFB's observation about the cistern-versus-fountain distinction sharpens this further -- the grace Christ gives does not sit inertly waiting to be consumed; it springs up from within, self-renewing and inexhaustible.

Missiologically, this passage contains a remarkable pattern: the person least expected to receive divine revelation is the first to receive it. A Samaritan woman with a complicated past gets the clearest messianic self-disclosure in John's Gospel, earlier and more explicit than anything given to Nicodemus (a respected Jewish leader, John 3) or the disciples themselves. And she becomes the first evangelist in John's narrative -- running back to her town to testify. Tyndale traces a miniature discipleship journey in the way she names Jesus across the conversation: "a Jew" (v.9), "Sir" (v.11, 15, 19), "a prophet" (v.19), a possible Messiah (v.29) -- and finally the townspeople reach "Savior of the world" (v.42). That progression, compressed into a single encounter, mirrors the journey of faith itself.

The woman's five husbands are sometimes allegorized as the five pagan deities the Samaritans worshiped (2 Kings 17:24-34). The allegorical reading has genuine historical grounding -- Gill documents extensively that the Samaritans were a mixed population descended from colonists who brought gods from Babylon, Cuthah, Ava, Hamath, and Sepharvaim, and rabbinic sources record accusations of worshiping fire, a dove-image on Gerizim, and the teraphim Jacob buried at Shechem. But the commentators themselves -- Clarke, Gill, Henry -- all treat the five husbands as literal husbands, and the power of the passage depends on Jesus knowing the actual contours of this particular woman's life rather than reciting national-religious allegory. Here is a person whose relational history is a series of losses. Whether through death, divorce, or abandonment, the text does not specify. Jesus offers her not another husband but living water -- a source of life that does not depend on anyone else's faithfulness.

## Relational Note

The relational architecture of this encounter repays close attention. Jesus does not categorize this woman before engaging her. He does not relate to her as "Samaritan," "woman," "sinner," or "outsider." He relates to her as a person who is thirsty -- both literally and otherwise -- and who is capable of the deepest worship.

His pastoral method, as JFB describes it, is to let her lead while he follows -- "but thus only the more effectually gaining His object." When she deflects to theology, he does not shut it down. When she evades about her husband, he does not shame her. Henry's observation about Jesus' technique deserves to be quoted in full: "Those who would win souls should make the best of them, whereby they may hope to work upon their good-nature; for, if they make the worst of them, they certainly exasperate their ill-nature." Jesus turns her evasion into an occasion for deeper truth.

His knowledge of her past is not wielded as judgment but offered as evidence that he sees her fully -- and remains present. This is the hinge of the entire encounter. She responds to being truly known not by running away but by running toward others: "Come, see a man who told me everything I ever did." The one thing that should have driven her deeper into isolation -- total exposure before a stranger -- instead becomes the first thing she tells her neighbors. Being fully known and not rejected is apparently so liberating that it transforms a social outcast into a missionary.

The text records the result: "Many of the Samaritans from that town believed in Jesus because of the woman's testimony." The least credentialed witness proves the most effective, because what she offers is not expertise but authenticity. She has been seen, and she cannot stop talking about it.

## Conceptual Note

Three theological threads converge in this passage and deserve to be distinguished.

**Living water and the indwelling Spirit.** Jesus' metaphor of living water is grounded in a specific Old Testament image: God as the fountain Israel has abandoned (Jer 2:13). Clarke notes that "living water" in both Eastern and Western antiquity always meant spring water -- flowing, moving, pure -- as opposed to stagnant cistern water. The Hebrew term mayim chayyim (Gen 26:19) denotes water "always flowing, and never ceasing" (Gill). JFB's distinction between two Greek words -- "well" in v.11 (phreatos, a static water source) and "fountain" in v.14 (pege, a spring) -- reveals the heart of the metaphor: Christ's gift transforms the soul from a cistern (external, always needing refilling from outside) into a fountain (internal, self-renewing, springing up of its own accord). John 7:37-39 confirms that the referent is the Holy Spirit.

**Worship in spirit and truth.** Jesus' declaration that true worshipers will worship "in spirit and truth" is not an argument against liturgy or sacred architecture; it is a claim about the nature of God and, therefore, the nature of encounter with God. "God is Spirit" (v.24) -- Clarke calls this "one of the first, the greatest, the most sublime, and necessary truths in the compass of nature." Because God is spirit, worship cannot be confined to a mountain or a temple; it must arise from the inner person under the influence of the Holy Spirit and in alignment with revealed truth. Tyndale's observation that a single Greek preposition governs both "spirit" and "truth" (literally "in spirit and truth") makes them a unified reality, not two separate checkboxes. This is simultaneously liberating (worship is everywhere) and intensifying (worship is nowhere that spirit and truth are absent).

**Salvation from the Jews.** Jesus' statement "salvation is from the Jews" (v.22) is the most direct affirmation of Jewish theological priority in the Fourth Gospel. JFB reads it as an insistence that salvation "had been revealed, prepared, deposited with a particular people, and must be sought in connection with, and as issuing from them." Clarke interprets "salvation" here as meaning "the Savior" himself (cf. Luke 2:30; Acts 4:12). Even as Jesus universalizes worship, he does not erase particularity. The universal gift comes through a particular history, a particular people, and a particular Messiah who identifies himself as Jewish ("we worship what we know"). The breadth of the Gospel's reach does not cancel the specificity of its source.

## Climate Note

Jacob's Well sits where the narrow valley of Shechem opens into a wide field -- "the ground given by Jacob to his son Joseph" (Clarke, citing Maundrell). A fresh stream runs between the field and the city, making the area "exceedingly verdant and fruitful." The well itself is dug in solid rock, roughly three yards in diameter and between sixty and one hundred and five feet deep, depending on the source (Gill cites forty cubits; Clarke cites Maundrell's measurement of thirty-five yards). A large church built by the Empress Irene once stood over it, of which only foundations remained by the time of Maundrell's visit. The city of Sychar was about thirty-four miles from Jerusalem (JFB). Its name may derive from a word meaning "drunken," a reference to Isaiah's charge against the Ephraimites (Isa 28:1, 3, 7, 8) -- confirmed by both Clarke and Gill.

The climate context from the database captures what daily life looked like: women's days were consumed by "the relentless labor of household production" -- grinding grain (one to three hours daily), drawing water from the village well (itself a social hub), baking bread, spinning wool. Drawing water was not a chore; it was the social event of the day, the place where "news was shared" and female kin networks supported each other. For this woman to remove herself from that daily rhythm was to cut herself off from the primary social infrastructure available to her.

The noon heat in the region could be fierce. Clarke notes the sixth hour (noon) accounts for three things: Christ's fatigue from traveling all morning, his thirst from the midday sun, and the disciples' errand to buy food at the ordinary Jewish dinner hour. Matthew Henry adds a telling detail -- the disciples walked into town "without any difficulty, when their Master sat down, and could not go a step further," suggesting that Jesus' physical frame was more sensitive to exhaustion than theirs. Into this scene of genuine human weariness walks a woman whose own exhaustion is not physical but social. Both are at the end of something. Both have come to a well.

## Modern Parallel

The U.S. Surgeon General's 2023 advisory on loneliness describes social isolation as a public health crisis comparable to smoking fifteen cigarettes a day. Average Americans report fewer close friends than at any point in recorded history. The loneliest demographics are young adults and those who have experienced repeated relational loss. The parallels to the woman at the well do not need to be forced -- they announce themselves.

She has had five husbands. In a culture where women could not initiate divorce, and where Clarke notes "divorces were very common among the Jews, so that a man put away his wife for any fault," she has likely been discarded repeatedly. She draws water at noon because the communal gathering at the well -- the ancient equivalent of the break room, the school pickup line, the neighborhood group chat -- has become unbearable. She has developed what we might call a "noon strategy": a pattern of behavior designed to minimize exposure, avoid being seen, and manage the pain of being known.

We all have noon strategies. We curate social media profiles that present an edited version of ourselves. We choose surface-level friendships that do not require real vulnerability. When conversations get personal, we deflect into abstract topics -- politics, theology, opinions about other people -- exactly as the woman does when Jesus touches the nerve of her marital history ("Let's talk about where people should worship"). Jesus refuses the deflection, not by shutting it down but by answering it so deeply that it opens back up into the personal. The theological question about worship becomes the most intimate revelation of his identity: "I who speak to you am he."

The deepest modern parallel may be this: the woman's breakthrough comes not when she receives new information but when she is fully known by another person -- and discovers that being known does not destroy her. She leads with the very thing that should have embarrassed her: "Come, see a man who told me everything I ever did." In a culture saturated with image management, the most radical thing a person can do is stop managing. Her witness is not a polished testimony; it is an honest question: "Could this be the Christ?" And it works. The people come. They listen. They believe. Authenticity is more persuasive than credentials.

## Key Questions

- Why does Jesus choose to have his most explicit messianic self-disclosure with someone from the most despised social group rather than with his own disciples or the Jewish religious establishment?
- The commentators disagree about whether the woman in v.15 is mocking Jesus or genuinely longing for what he offers. What difference does each reading make for how we understand the passage?
- Jesus says "salvation is from the Jews" in the same conversation where he dismantles Jewish exclusivism about worship. How do particularity and universality coexist here?
- What does "worship in spirit and truth" mean practically? Is it about the absence of liturgy and sacred space, or about something deeper?
- The woman tells her town, "Come, see a man who told me everything I ever did." Why is being fully known the thing she leads with? What does this suggest about the relationship between exposure and freedom?
- Clarke and Pearce argue the woman may not have been immoral at all -- that the five husbands may reflect abandonment rather than promiscuity. How does our assumption about her character shape what we take from the passage?
- How does the progression of the woman's names for Jesus -- Jew, Sir, Prophet, possible Messiah, Savior of the world -- map onto stages of coming to faith?

## Preaching Angles

- **The Noon Strategy: How We Avoid Being Known** (people carrying shame) -- The woman arranges her day around avoiding others. We arrange our lives around avoiding vulnerability. Jesus meets us at the well we thought no one would visit.
- **The Cistern and the Fountain: External Religion vs. Internal Renewal** (those exhausted by religious performance) -- JFB's distinction between the cistern (external, always running out) and the fountain (internal, self-renewing) offers a frame for spiritual burnout and the difference between performing faith and possessing it.
- **Five Husbands and Living Water: When Human Love Is Not Enough** (those with relational wounds) -- She has sought life in five relationships and found none of them sufficient. Jesus offers a source that does not depend on another person's faithfulness.
- **The Unlikely Evangelist: Why Broken People Are the Best Witnesses** (those who feel disqualified from ministry) -- The least credentialed person in the story becomes its most effective missionary. Authenticity outweighs credentials.
- **Neither Here Nor There: Worship Beyond Walls** (those deconstructing institutional faith) -- Jesus neither endorses Gerizim nor Jerusalem as the permanent location of God's presence. Worship is liberated from geography -- but intensified in its demands.
- **The Prophet Who Made the Best of Her: Christ's Gentle Confrontation** (pastors, counselors, anyone who must speak difficult truths) -- Henry's observation that Jesus turned evasion into confession by putting "a better construction than it would well bear" on her words models a confrontation style that opens doors rather than slamming them.
