# The Valley of Dry Bones (Ezekiel 37:1-14) — Side-by-Side Comparison

## GROUP A: Control (No Commentary)

# The Valley of Dry Bones: Can These Bones Live?

**EZK 37:1-37:14**

## Summary

The hand of the LORD is on Ezekiel, and the Spirit sets him down in the middle of a valley — and the valley is full of bones. Not fresh bones; dry bones. These bones have been dead so long that every trace of flesh, every membrane of sinew, every drop of moisture has evaporated. Whatever killed these people happened a long time ago, and no one buried them. They have been baking in the sun, bleaching to white, drying to dust. This is death at its most irreversible.

God asks Ezekiel a question that is either absurd or the most important question in Scripture: "Son of man, can these bones live?" Ezekiel's answer is theologically masterful: "Sovereign LORD, you alone know." He does not say yes (which would be presumptuous) or no (which would deny God's power). He places the entire question back in God's hands.

God tells Ezekiel to prophesy to the bones — to preach a sermon to the dead. And Ezekiel does it. He speaks to dry bones, and there is a rattling — the bones coming together, bone to bone. Then sinew, then flesh, then skin. But they are not alive. They are corpses — reassembled but not reanimated. Then God tells Ezekiel to prophesy to the breath (ruach — the same word as "spirit" and "wind"): "Come, breath, from the four winds and breathe into these slain, that they may live." The breath enters them, and they stand up — a vast army.

God explains the vision: "These bones are the whole house of Israel. They say, 'Our bones are dried up and our hope is gone; we are cut off.'" The vision is about national resurrection — the restoration of a people who have given up, who consider themselves dead. God promises to open their graves, to bring them back to the land, to put his Spirit in them. The passage is not primarily about individual resurrection after death (though later theology will extend it there); it is about the resurrection of a community that has lost the will to live.

## Significance

The valley of dry bones is arguably the most powerful image of hope in the entire Bible. Its genius lies in the specificity of the death it describes: not recent, not partial, but complete — dry, scattered, ancient. The vision insists that there is no condition so terminal that God cannot reverse it. It has been adopted by liberation movements, recovery communities, civil rights leaders, and anyone who has ever looked at a situation and concluded that it was beyond saving. The passage's influence on Christian resurrection theology is enormous: if God can raise a valley of dry bones, then individual resurrection is certainly within the range of divine power.

## Relational Note

The relational center of this passage is between God and a despairing people. "Our bones are dried up and our hope is gone; we are cut off" — this is the self-diagnosis of a community that has given up on itself. They have internalized their death. They no longer believe they can live. And God's response is not to argue with their assessment but to act: "I will open your graves... I will put my Spirit in you, and you will live." The passage suggests that when hope is truly dead — not merely discouraged but dead — only divine action can restore it. Human effort, positive thinking, strategic planning — none of these address the problem of dry bones. Only the breath of God does.

## Conceptual Note

The passage operates on two levels of creation theology. First, the reassembly of the bones into bodies echoes the formation of Adam from the dust. Second, the breathing of life into the bodies directly parallels Genesis 2:7, where God breathes into Adam's nostrils. Ezekiel's vision is a new creation — not the creation of something from nothing but the re-creation of something from ruin. The distinction between the two prophetic acts — prophesying to the bones (which produces form) and prophesying to the breath (which produces life) — suggests that restoration requires both structural and spiritual renewal. You can reassemble the institution, but without the Spirit, it remains a corpse.

## Climate Note

The "valley" (biq'ah) in the vision may refer to the broad, flat valleys of Mesopotamia where Ezekiel lived in exile — a landscape very different from the narrow wadis of Judah. Battlefields in the ancient Near East were often left unburied, particularly when the defeated army had no surviving community to perform burial rites. The sun and wind of the Mesopotamian plain would rapidly desiccate exposed remains. The "four winds" from which the breath comes reflect ancient Near Eastern cosmology, where the four cardinal directions were associated with the totality of the world — God summons life from everywhere, from every direction.

## Modern Parallel

The valley of dry bones has been adopted as a text of hope by African American communities since the era of slavery. The spiritual "Dem Bones" (or "Dry Bones") is one of the best-known songs in American music, and its message — that God can reassemble what has been scattered and revive what has been killed — spoke directly to communities that had been systematically dismembered: families torn apart by the slave trade, cultures destroyed by forced assimilation, bodies broken by labor and violence. "Can these bones live?" was not a theoretical question; it was a survival question.

The passage also speaks powerfully to communities devastated by addiction. Recovery communities often describe their pre-recovery state in language that echoes the dry bones: "Our hope was gone. We were cut off." The twelve-step tradition's emphasis on a "higher power" resonates with Ezekiel's theology: the breath that revives the bones does not come from within them. Life is breathed in from outside. The addict in the depths of their addiction is, in Ezekiel's terms, a dry bone — not partially alive, not mostly dead, but dead dead. And the promise of the passage is that even dead dead is not beyond the reach of the breath of God.

The two-stage resurrection — first form (bones, sinew, flesh, skin), then life (breath) — maps onto the experience of institutional rebuilding. After a collapse (a bankruptcy, a scandal, a natural disaster), the first stage is structural: rebuilding the organization, rewriting the bylaws, reopening the doors. But the structure without the spirit is a corpse. The second stage — the harder stage — is reviving the mission, the culture, the animating purpose. Many institutions complete stage one and never reach stage two. They are bodies without breath.

- What does Ezekiel's answer — 'Sovereign LORD, you alone know' — teach us about the posture of faith before impossible situations?
- Why does the vision require two prophetic acts (to the bones and to the breath) — what does this two-stage process reveal about restoration?
- How does the identification of the bones as 'the whole house of Israel' change the passage from an individual resurrection text to a communal one?
- What modern communities or situations could be described as 'valleys of dry bones' — and what would it look like for them to receive breath?
- How does this passage challenge the assumption that some situations are genuinely beyond hope?

The wind itself brings no life into dead bodies." What enters the corpses is "that divine breath of life which pervades all nature, giving and sustaining the life of all creatures" (cf. [ENRICHED: Keil & Delitzsch are emphatic on this point: the word harugiym in v.9 "indisputably proves that Ezekiel was not shown the resurrection of all the dead, but simply the raising to life of Israel, which had been swept away by a violent death." However, K&D also argue that the vision does contain a prophecy of bodily resurrection — but as background, not foreground: "the figurative meaning constitutes the foreground, and the full, literal meaning of the words the background of the prophetic vision, and the fulfilment advances from the figurative to the literal meaning." # Ezekiel 37:1-14 -- The Valley of Dry Bones

**Book:** Ezekiel | **Chapter:** 37 | **Verses:** 1-14
**Translation:** BSB (Berean Standard Bible)

## Passage Text

> 1: The hand of the LORD was upon me, and He brought me out by His Spirit and set me down in the middle of the valley, and it was full of bones.
> 2: He led me all around among them, and I saw a great many bones on the floor of the valley, and indeed, they were very dry.
> 3: Then He asked me, "Son of man, can these bones come to life?" "O Lord GOD," I replied, "only You know."
> 4: And He said to me, "Prophesy concerning these bones and tell them, 'Dry bones, hear the word of the LORD!
> 5: This is what the Lord GOD says to these bones: I will cause breath to enter you, and you will come to life.
> 6: I will attach tendons to you and make flesh grow upon you and cover you with skin. I will put breath within you so that you will come to life. Then you will know that I am the LORD.'"
> 7: So I prophesied as I had been commanded. And as I prophesied, there was suddenly a noise, a rattling, and the bones came together, bone to bone.
> 8: As I looked on, tendons appeared on them, flesh grew, and skin covered them; but there was no breath in them.
> 9: Then He said to me, "Prophesy to the breath; prophesy, son of man, and tell the breath that this is what the Lord GOD says: Come from the four winds, O breath, and breathe into these slain, so that they may live!"
> 10: So I prophesied as He had commanded me, and the breath entered them, and they came to life and stood on their feet -- a vast army.
> 11: Then He said to me, "Son of man, these bones are the whole house of Israel. Look, they are saying, 'Our bones are dried up, and our hope has perished; we are cut off.'
> 12: Therefore prophesy and tell them that this is what the Lord GOD says: 'O My people, I will open your graves and bring you up from them, and I will bring you back to the land of Israel.
> 13: Then you, My people, will know that I am the LORD, when I open your graves and bring you up from them.
> 14: I will put My Spirit in you and you will live, and I will settle you in your own land. Then you will know that I, the LORD, have spoken, and I will do it, declares the LORD.'"

---

## Summary

God takes Ezekiel -- a priest who received his prophetic calling in Babylon, and who bore some of the most physically and emotionally grueling sign-acts in all of Scripture -- and sets him down in a valley carpeted with human bones. Not a tidy ossuary. A killing field, bleached and silent, bones disjointed and scattered under open sky. God walks him through them, slowly, so he sees the scale of it, and then asks a question that hangs in the air: "Can these bones live?"

Ezekiel's answer is a masterclass in honest faith: "Lord GOD, You know." He refuses to say no -- because he knows God's power. He refuses to say yes -- because he can see what's in front of him. He puts the question back where it belongs.

What follows unfolds in two deliberate stages, echoing the creation of humanity in Genesis 2. First, Ezekiel prophesies to the bones, and they reassemble -- tendons, flesh, skin knitting together with a rattling sound that must have been extraordinary. But these reconstituted bodies lie there without breath; they are corpses, not people. The physical structure alone is not enough. So God commands a second prophecy, this time addressed to the *ruach* -- the breath, wind, spirit (the Hebrew word carries all three meanings simultaneously). Ezekiel calls the breath from the four winds, it enters the slain, and they rise to their feet: a vast army.

Then comes the interpretation. God is explicit: these bones represent the whole house of Israel -- all twelve tribes, not just Judah -- in its condition of national death during exile. The people have been saying, "Our bones are dried up, our hope has perished, we are cut off." God's answer is not gentle reassurance but an act of sovereign reversal: He will open their graves, raise them, infuse them with His Spirit, and resettle them in their own land.

## Significance

This passage operates on multiple levels at once, and scholarly commentary across centuries has debated the relationship between them. At its core, there are three interlocking significances:

**1. National Restoration from Exile (Primary)**
Keil and Delitzsch are emphatic: the vision's primary meaning is the raising of the nation of Israel from its state of political and spiritual death in exile. The bones are called *harugiym* -- "slain" -- not simply "dead," which specifies that this is a people destroyed by violence (sword, famine, siege), not merely deceased of natural causes. The valley represents Israel annihilated as a functioning nation, and the resurrection represents God's sovereign act of restoration. Clarke maps the two-stage process onto specific historical events: the decree of Cyrus (the initial noise and shaking), the edict of Darius (flesh and skin), and the mission of Nehemiah (the breath of life that made Israel stand as a functioning community again).

**2. The Power of the Prophetic Word**
Jamieson-Fausset-Brown observe that God gives extraordinary honor to the divine word "even in the mouth of a man." The entire resurrection happens through prophesying -- speaking God's word to impossibility. The passage is structured so that the prophet's obedient proclamation is the instrument through which divine power flows. Matthew Henry draws the connection to ministry: "God's grace can save souls without our preaching, but our preaching cannot save them without God's grace."

**3. Indirect Witness to Bodily Resurrection**
Keil and Delitzsch argue carefully that while the vision's direct meaning is national restoration, its "figurative meaning constitutes the foreground, and the full, literal meaning of the words the background." The vision takes the possibility of bodily resurrection as its operative metaphor, which would be incoherent if resurrection were not considered real. As Jamieson-Fausset-Brown put it: the chapter "does not directly prove the resurrection of the dead" but "does so indirectly; for it takes for granted the future fact as one recognized by believing Jews, and so made the image of their national restoration." Jerome's defense remains pointed: you cannot use a resurrection as a convincing metaphor if no one believes resurrection is possible.

## Relational Note

There is something striking about the way God handles Ezekiel in this vision. He does not just show him the valley from a distance. He walks him through it -- "all around among them" -- forcing close-quarters encounter with the scale and finality of death. Only after Ezekiel has fully absorbed the horror does God ask the question.

And the question itself is relational, not rhetorical. "Can these bones live?" is an invitation into honest dialogue. God is not looking for a correct theological answer. He is looking for Ezekiel to locate himself -- his faith, his doubt, his limits -- in relation to divine power. Ezekiel's response, "Lord GOD, You know," is neither passive resignation nor triumphant certainty. It is the posture of someone who has learned, through years of costly obedience, to hold the tension between what he sees and what he believes about who God is.

This matters for anyone who has ever stood in front of a situation that looks completely dead and been asked to still believe. The passage does not demand that you feel hopeful. It demands that you locate the question of possibility where it belongs -- with God, not with your assessment of the situation.

## Conceptual Note: *Ruach* -- The Word That Holds It All Together

The single most important word in this passage is *ruach*, and it is doing extraordinary work. The Hebrew word means "breath," "wind," and "spirit" simultaneously -- and Ezekiel exploits all three meanings in rapid succession.

Clarke's commentary traces the semantic range: in verse 5, *ruach* refers to the animating breath of life; in verse 9, it carries the sense of wind summoned from the four directions; in verse 14, it becomes the Spirit of God Himself. As Clarke writes: "Soul is its proper meaning in this vision, where it refers to the bones... Here *ruach* is taken for the Holy Ghost."

Keil and Delitzsch push further: the *ruach* that enters the bodies in verses 9-10 is not merely air but "that divine breath of life which pervades all nature, giving and sustaining the life of all creatures" -- and the expression "breathe into these slain" deliberately echoes Genesis 2:7, where God breathed life into Adam. The two-stage process (first physical assembly, then breath) mirrors creation itself. Theodoret noticed this centuries earlier: "For as the body of our forefather Adam was first moulded, and then the soul was thus breathed into it; so here also both combined in fitting harmony."

This layered meaning is not accidental ambiguity. It is theological architecture. Physical breath, cosmic wind, and divine Spirit are presented as a single continuum. The God who moves the wind also animates the body also transforms the soul. There is no compartmentalization -- no gap between "natural" and "supernatural." Life at every level comes from the same source.

## Climate Note

Ezekiel delivers this vision from exile in Babylon -- a flat, canal-laced agricultural region utterly unlike the rocky highlands of Judah. The exiled community lived in mud-brick settlements along irrigation canals, laboring to maintain ditches and tend crops in heavy clay soil. They ate barley bread and dates, conducted commerce in Aramaic, and gathered in the evenings to hear an elder read from scrolls, anchored by Sabbath observance as the chief marker of identity in a foreign land.

The emotional weight of exile pervades verse 11: "Our bones are dried up, and our hope has perished; we are cut off." These are not abstract theological sentiments. They are the words of a community that has watched its temple burn, its city demolished, its people scattered, and its children growing up speaking someone else's language. The despair is existential and communal -- not "I have lost hope" but "we are cut off." The Hebrew *nigzarnu lanu* means something like "we are severed, as far as we are concerned" -- the sense of being amputated from the living body of their own national and spiritual life.

Against this backdrop, the promise of verse 14 -- "I will settle you in your own land" -- is not a vague spiritual comfort. It is the most concrete, embodied promise imaginable: You will have land under your feet again. Your own land. And it will be God who puts you there.

## Modern Parallel

The exile experience maps directly onto modern displaced and diaspora communities -- Palestinian refugees in Jordan, Tibetans in India, Uyghurs scattered across Central Asia, or any immigrant community wrestling with how to maintain identity when homeland, institutions, and autonomy have been stripped away.

But the Valley of Dry Bones speaks to a specific subset of that experience: the moment when a community stops believing it can be reconstituted. Not the early days of displacement, when return feels imminent, but the generation-deep exhaustion where the communal language starts to die, where children assimilate, where elders stop telling the old stories because the pain of remembering outweighs the hope of returning. "Our hope has perished; we are cut off."

This also maps, at an individual level, onto the experience of anyone who has watched something essential die -- a marriage, a career, a church community, a sense of purpose -- and reached the point where the death feels not just current but permanent. The bones are not just dead; they are *very dry*. This is not recent loss. This is loss that has had time to bleach.

The passage does not minimize the deadness. God does not say, "It's not that bad." He walks Ezekiel through the full reality first. The promise comes *after* the honest reckoning, not instead of it. And the mechanism of restoration is not human effort or positive thinking but divine initiative: "I will open your graves... I will put My Spirit in you... I will settle you in your own land." The agency belongs entirely to God, and the passage insists that this is precisely why hope is warranted -- because hope was never supposed to be grounded in human capacity to begin with.

## Key Questions

1. **Why does God walk Ezekiel through the bones before asking the question?** What does it mean that honest encounter with the full scale of death precedes the promise of life?

2. **Why is the restoration in two stages -- physical reassembly, then breath -- rather than instantaneous?** What does the intermediate state (bodies without breath) signify? Jamieson-Fausset-Brown suggest it may reflect a stage of outward restoration without inward transformation -- "a man may assume all the semblances of spiritual life, yet have none, and so be dead before God." What does that look like in practice?

3. **Ezekiel is told to prophesy to the *breath* itself.** What does it mean to speak to the Spirit of God as though your words could summon it? What is the relationship between prophetic speech (or prayer) and divine action?

4. **The bones are called "the whole house of Israel" -- all twelve tribes, not just Judah.** Given the centuries-old division of the kingdom, what is the significance of God claiming the *whole* house at this moment of maximum national death?

5. **Verse 11 records what Israel is *saying*: "Our bones are dried up, our hope has perished."** God does not dispute their assessment of their condition. He disputes their conclusion. What is the difference between an accurate diagnosis and a final verdict?

## Preaching Angles

### 1. "Only You Know" -- The Posture of Honest Faith
Ezekiel's answer to God's question is neither false optimism nor defeated cynicism. "O Lord GOD, only You know" is a model for holding faith and realism together. Preach this to a congregation that has been told faith means always being certain, or that doubt is always the enemy. Sometimes the most faithful response is to refuse to answer a question that only God can answer.

### 2. Bodies Without Breath -- The Danger of Structural Restoration Without Spiritual Life
The intermediate stage (v. 8) -- bodies reassembled but breathless -- is its own sermon. Gill and Jamieson-Fausset-Brown both notice the warning: you can have the outward form of life (sinews, flesh, skin -- institutions, programs, orthodoxy) and still be a corpse. The structure is necessary but not sufficient. This speaks to any church, organization, or individual that has rebuilt the visible architecture of faith but has not yet asked for the breath.

### 3. Speaking to the Wind -- The Prophetic Imagination
God tells Ezekiel to prophesy *to the breath*. Not to the bones this time, but to the animating force itself. This is the moment where human speech and divine power intersect most dramatically. For preachers: there are times when the most important thing you can say is directed not at the congregation but at the Spirit -- "Come from the four winds, and breathe."

### 4. "We Are Cut Off" vs. "O My People" -- God's Counternarrative
Israel's self-assessment in v. 11 is devastating: dried up, hopeless, severed. God's response in v. 12 begins with two words that overwrite the entire narrative: "My people." The covenant relationship has not been dissolved by exile. The passage does not argue against despair; it reasserts identity. You are still Mine. Preach this to anyone whose suffering has made them question whether they still belong to God.

### 5. The God Who Opens Graves -- Sovereign Initiative in Hopeless Situations
The verbs in vv. 12-14 are all first person: I will open, I will bring, I will put, I will settle. There is no command here for Israel to do anything. Restoration is presented as entirely God's work. This is not passivity on Israel's part but a recognition that some things are so dead that only the Author of life can reverse them. Preach this against the prosperity-gospel implication that spiritual life is something you generate through sufficient effort.
