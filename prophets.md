# Prophets Narrative Unit Samples

Four selections from the prophetic books.

---

## Isaiah — Isaiah's Call: The Throne Room Vision (Isaiah 6:1-6:13)

### Summary

In the year King Uzziah died — a ruler whose fifty-two-year reign was the only stability most Israelites had ever known — Isaiah finds himself standing in the throne room of God. The scene is staggering: seraphim with six wings crying "Holy, holy, holy," smoke filling the temple, the doorposts shaking. This is not a gentle devotional moment. It is the collision of a finite human being with infinite holiness, and Isaiah's first response is not awe but terror: "Woe is me! I am ruined."

What happens next is one of the most compressed salvation narratives in all of Scripture. A seraph takes a live coal from the altar and presses it to Isaiah's lips — pain and purification fused into a single gesture. His guilt is removed, his sin atoned for. And then comes the voice: "Whom shall I send? And who will go for us?" Isaiah's answer — "Here am I. Send me!" — is not bravado. It is the response of a man who has just been unmade and remade in the same breath.

But the commission that follows is devastating. God tells Isaiah that his preaching will make the people more deaf, more blind, more calloused. He is being sent on a mission that will, by every external measure, fail. The passage ends with an image of a felled tree whose stump still holds a "holy seed" — the smallest possible thread of hope woven into a narrative of comprehensive judgment.

### Significance

This passage establishes the theological architecture for the entire book of Isaiah. The triple "holy" is the only attribute of God repeated three times in Scripture, suggesting that holiness is not one characteristic among many but the defining reality of who God is. Isaiah's response — immediate confession of his own uncleanness and the uncleanness of his people — reveals that genuine encounter with God always produces simultaneous awareness of divine glory and human brokenness.

The commission to preach a message that hardens rather than softens is one of the most theologically difficult passages in the prophets. It raises the question of whether prophetic speech is primarily about changing the audience or about faithfully bearing witness regardless of outcome — a question that reverberates through every generation of truth-tellers.

### Relational Note

Isaiah's vision occurs at a moment of profound national uncertainty. The death of Uzziah was not just the loss of a king but the collapse of an era. Into this vacuum, Isaiah discovers that the real throne — the one that matters — is still occupied. The relational dynamic here is between a community in crisis and a God who is not absent but terrifyingly present. Isaiah's willingness to go, even knowing his message will be rejected, models a kind of faithfulness that does not require success as its validation.

### Conceptual Note

The theology of holiness in this passage is not moralistic — it is ontological. Holiness describes the fundamental otherness of God, the gap between Creator and creature that can only be bridged by divine initiative (the coal from the altar). The commission challenges the modern assumption that effective communication always produces positive results. Sometimes the most faithful speech is the speech that exposes the audience's refusal to hear.

### Climate Note

The throne room vision takes place in the Jerusalem temple, likely during one of the great festivals. The physical setting — incense smoke, trembling thresholds, the cry of the seraphim — would have been experienced as a sensory overload in the already-charged atmosphere of ancient Near Eastern worship. Jerusalem in Uzziah's death year (c. 740 BCE) was a prosperous city beginning to feel the seismic pressure of Assyrian expansion from the northeast, lending the vision an urgency beyond the merely spiritual.

### Modern Parallel

Consider the investigative journalist who uncovers systemic corruption in a beloved institution — a hospital system falsifying patient outcomes, a school district covering up abuse. Like Isaiah, they experience a kind of "throne room moment" when they see the evidence clearly for the first time: the gap between what the institution claims to be and what it actually is. Their first response is often a version of "woe is me" — they are implicated too, part of the system they must now expose.

The commission to speak a message people refuse to hear maps directly onto the experience of climate scientists in the 1990s and 2000s, who presented increasingly urgent data to audiences that became more resistant, not less. The "hardening" dynamic Isaiah describes is not magical — it is the well-documented psychological phenomenon of motivated reasoning, where exposure to threatening information deepens denial rather than producing change.

Isaiah's "here am I, send me" is also the posture of the whistleblower who knows the personal cost — professional exile, public ridicule, legal harassment — and steps forward anyway, not because they expect to win but because the alternative (silence in the face of what they have seen) has become impossible.

### Key Questions

- What does it mean that Isaiah's lips had to be purified before he could speak for God — is there a connection between personal transformation and public vocation?
- How do we make sense of a God who commissions a prophet to deliver a message that will make the audience worse, not better?
- In what ways does the death of Uzziah function as more than a chronological marker — what dies when a long era of stability ends?
- What is the relationship between seeing God's holiness and seeing your own uncleanness — can you have one without the other?
- How does Isaiah's immediate volunteering ('here am I, send me') challenge or confirm our assumptions about how divine calling works?
- What does the image of the stump and the holy seed suggest about how hope functions in a context of overwhelming judgment?

## Ezekiel — The Valley of Dry Bones: Can These Bones Live? (Ezekiel 37:1-37:14)

### Summary

The hand of the LORD is on Ezekiel, and the Spirit sets him down in the middle of a valley — and the valley is full of bones. Not fresh bones; dry bones. These bones have been dead so long that every trace of flesh, every membrane of sinew, every drop of moisture has evaporated. Whatever killed these people happened a long time ago, and no one buried them. They have been baking in the sun, bleaching to white, drying to dust. This is death at its most irreversible.

God asks Ezekiel a question that is either absurd or the most important question in Scripture: "Son of man, can these bones live?" Ezekiel's answer is theologically masterful: "Sovereign LORD, you alone know." He does not say yes (which would be presumptuous) or no (which would deny God's power). He places the entire question back in God's hands.

God tells Ezekiel to prophesy to the bones — to preach a sermon to the dead. And Ezekiel does it. He speaks to dry bones, and there is a rattling — the bones coming together, bone to bone. Then sinew, then flesh, then skin. But they are not alive. They are corpses — reassembled but not reanimated. Then God tells Ezekiel to prophesy to the breath (ruach — the same word as "spirit" and "wind"): "Come, breath, from the four winds and breathe into these slain, that they may live." The breath enters them, and they stand up — a vast army.

God explains the vision: "These bones are the whole house of Israel. They say, 'Our bones are dried up and our hope is gone; we are cut off.'" The vision is about national resurrection — the restoration of a people who have given up, who consider themselves dead. God promises to open their graves, to bring them back to the land, to put his Spirit in them. The passage is not primarily about individual resurrection after death (though later theology will extend it there); it is about the resurrection of a community that has lost the will to live.

### Significance

The valley of dry bones is arguably the most powerful image of hope in the entire Bible. Its genius lies in the specificity of the death it describes: not recent, not partial, but complete — dry, scattered, ancient. The vision insists that there is no condition so terminal that God cannot reverse it. It has been adopted by liberation movements, recovery communities, civil rights leaders, and anyone who has ever looked at a situation and concluded that it was beyond saving. The passage's influence on Christian resurrection theology is enormous: if God can raise a valley of dry bones, then individual resurrection is certainly within the range of divine power.

### Relational Note

The relational center of this passage is between God and a despairing people. "Our bones are dried up and our hope is gone; we are cut off" — this is the self-diagnosis of a community that has given up on itself. They have internalized their death. They no longer believe they can live. And God's response is not to argue with their assessment but to act: "I will open your graves... I will put my Spirit in you, and you will live." The passage suggests that when hope is truly dead — not merely discouraged but dead — only divine action can restore it. Human effort, positive thinking, strategic planning — none of these address the problem of dry bones. Only the breath of God does.

### Conceptual Note

The passage operates on two levels of creation theology. First, the reassembly of the bones into bodies echoes the formation of Adam from the dust. Second, the breathing of life into the bodies directly parallels Genesis 2:7, where God breathes into Adam's nostrils. Ezekiel's vision is a new creation — not the creation of something from nothing but the re-creation of something from ruin. The distinction between the two prophetic acts — prophesying to the bones (which produces form) and prophesying to the breath (which produces life) — suggests that restoration requires both structural and spiritual renewal. You can reassemble the institution, but without the Spirit, it remains a corpse.

### Climate Note

The "valley" (biq'ah) in the vision may refer to the broad, flat valleys of Mesopotamia where Ezekiel lived in exile — a landscape very different from the narrow wadis of Judah. Battlefields in the ancient Near East were often left unburied, particularly when the defeated army had no surviving community to perform burial rites. The sun and wind of the Mesopotamian plain would rapidly desiccate exposed remains. The "four winds" from which the breath comes reflect ancient Near Eastern cosmology, where the four cardinal directions were associated with the totality of the world — God summons life from everywhere, from every direction.

### Modern Parallel

The valley of dry bones has been adopted as a text of hope by African American communities since the era of slavery. The spiritual "Dem Bones" (or "Dry Bones") is one of the best-known songs in American music, and its message — that God can reassemble what has been scattered and revive what has been killed — spoke directly to communities that had been systematically dismembered: families torn apart by the slave trade, cultures destroyed by forced assimilation, bodies broken by labor and violence. "Can these bones live?" was not a theoretical question; it was a survival question.

The passage also speaks powerfully to communities devastated by addiction. Recovery communities often describe their pre-recovery state in language that echoes the dry bones: "Our hope was gone. We were cut off." The twelve-step tradition's emphasis on a "higher power" resonates with Ezekiel's theology: the breath that revives the bones does not come from within them. Life is breathed in from outside. The addict in the depths of their addiction is, in Ezekiel's terms, a dry bone — not partially alive, not mostly dead, but dead dead. And the promise of the passage is that even dead dead is not beyond the reach of the breath of God.

The two-stage resurrection — first form (bones, sinew, flesh, skin), then life (breath) — maps onto the experience of institutional rebuilding. After a collapse (a bankruptcy, a scandal, a natural disaster), the first stage is structural: rebuilding the organization, rewriting the bylaws, reopening the doors. But the structure without the spirit is a corpse. The second stage — the harder stage — is reviving the mission, the culture, the animating purpose. Many institutions complete stage one and never reach stage two. They are bodies without breath.

### Key Questions

- What does Ezekiel's answer — 'Sovereign LORD, you alone know' — teach us about the posture of faith before impossible situations?
- Why does the vision require two prophetic acts (to the bones and to the breath) — what does this two-stage process reveal about restoration?
- How does the identification of the bones as 'the whole house of Israel' change the passage from an individual resurrection text to a communal one?
- What modern communities or situations could be described as 'valleys of dry bones' — and what would it look like for them to receive breath?
- How does this passage challenge the assumption that some situations are genuinely beyond hope?

## Jonah — The Prophet Who Was Angry That Grace Worked (Jonah 4:1-4:11)

### Summary

Here is the real climax of Jonah, and it is not what anyone expected. God has just spared the greatest city in the world. A hundred and twenty thousand people have been saved. By any measure, this is the most successful prophetic mission in Israel's history. And Jonah is furious. "Isn't this what I said, LORD, when I was still at home? That is what I tried to forestall by fleeing to Tarshish. I knew that you are a gracious and compassionate God, slow to anger and abounding in love, a God who relents from sending calamity." Jonah finally reveals why he ran. He was not afraid of Nineveh. He was afraid that God would be merciful.

The phrase Jonah quotes — "gracious and compassionate, slow to anger, abounding in love" — is the great creedal formula of Exodus 34:6-7, the moment when God revealed the divine nature to Moses on Sinai. It is the most important self-description of God in the Hebrew Bible. And Jonah uses it as a complaint. He throws God's own self-revelation back in God's face as if it were an accusation. "I knew you would do this. I knew you couldn't help yourself. You are constitutionally incapable of staying angry."

God's response is a question: "Is it right for you to be angry?" Then God provides a plant to shade Jonah, and Jonah is delighted. Then God sends a worm that kills the plant, and Jonah is distraught — so distraught that he asks to die. Over a plant. God delivers the final line of the book, and it is devastating: "You have been concerned about this plant, though you did not tend it or make it grow. It sprang up overnight and died overnight. And should I not have concern for the great city of Nineveh, in which there are more than a hundred and twenty thousand people who cannot tell their right hand from their left — and also many animals?"

The book ends with a question. There is no answer recorded. No resolution. No statement that Jonah learned his lesson. The question hangs in the air, directed past Jonah to every reader who has ever valued their own comfort more than someone else's survival.

### Significance

Jonah 4 is the theological climax of the entire book and one of the most important passages in the Hebrew Bible for understanding the nature of God. Jonah's complaint reveals that God's mercy is not a deviation from the divine nature — it IS the divine nature. God is not merciful despite being God; God is merciful because that is what it means to be God. The Exodus 34 creedal formula, which is the most repeated description of God in the Old Testament, is here turned into a complaint by a prophet who finds divine mercy intolerable when applied to the wrong people.

The book's ending — an unanswered question — is a brilliant literary and theological choice. It refuses to let the reader off the hook. If Jonah had repented and learned his lesson, the reader could nod approvingly and move on. Instead, the question lingers: do you care more about your comfort than about other people's lives? The book of Jonah is the Bible's most effective device for exposing the selfishness that hides beneath religious orthodoxy.

### Relational Note

Jonah's anger at God's mercy is the anger of someone who has defined their identity by being against something — and who discovers that God is not against it in the same way. Jonah's theological identity was partly built on the conviction that Nineveh deserved destruction. When God spared Nineveh, Jonah did not just lose an argument; he lost a piece of himself. This is why he asks to die. It is not hyperbole. It is the existential crisis of someone whose worldview has been shattered by grace.

The plant episode reveals the grotesque disproportion in Jonah's empathy. He can weep over a withered bush — something he did not create, did not tend, and that existed for one day — while remaining unmoved by the potential destruction of a hundred and twenty thousand human beings. God's final question exposes this disproportion without condemning it directly, which is more devastating than any condemnation could be.

### Conceptual Note

The theological concept at the heart of Jonah 4 is what might be called "the scandal of grace" — the idea that divine mercy, when fully understood, is always offensive to someone. Grace is not a problem when it is applied to us. It becomes a problem when it is applied to people we believe don't deserve it. Jonah represents the permanent human temptation to accept grace as a personal entitlement while opposing it as a universal principle.

The unanswered question at the end of the book has been debated for millennia. Some scholars see it as a literary device (the reader must answer for themselves). Others see it as a genuine open question within the narrative (Jonah never does answer). Either way, it performs a theological function: it places the reader in Jonah's position and asks whether they share his priorities. Do you care more about your plant or about Nineveh's people?

### Climate Note

The plant that God provides (Hebrew: qiqayon) is probably the castor oil plant (Ricinus communis), which grows rapidly in hot climates and can produce broad, shade-giving leaves within days. The extreme heat that follows the plant's death reflects the harsh climate of the region — temperatures in Mesopotamia and the eastern Mediterranean can exceed 110 degrees Fahrenheit, and shade is not a luxury but a necessity for survival.

The reference to "a hundred and twenty thousand people who cannot tell their right hand from their left" is usually interpreted as referring to children or to the general population's moral ignorance (they don't know right from wrong). The mention of "many animals" at the very end is the book's final ironic touch — even the animals, which have no moral agency whatsoever, are included in God's concern. The God of Jonah cares about cattle. Jonah cares about a bush.

### Modern Parallel

A church in a rural American town spends years praying for the downfall of a corrupt local politician who has hurt their community, enriched himself at public expense, and openly mocked their faith. Then the politician has a heart attack, nearly dies, and in the hospital room has what appears to be a genuine conversion experience. He repents publicly, resigns his position, and starts attending a church — a different church, but still. He gives interviews about God's grace. He weeps on camera.

And the praying church is furious. Not because they doubt the conversion. Because they believe it. God answered their prayers, but the wrong way. They wanted justice. They got grace. And grace, applied to the person who hurt them, feels like a betrayal.

One church member compares the situation to his flower garden, which the politician's construction project destroyed years ago. "I cared more about those flowers than God seems to care about what that man did to this town." The comparison is absurd, and he knows it, but the emotion is real. He is Jonah, mourning his plant while God is concerned about a city.

The book of Jonah ends with a question because it is a question every generation must answer for itself: would you rather be right, or would you rather live in a world where grace is real? You cannot have both. If grace is real, it will eventually be given to someone you think doesn't deserve it. And when that happens, will you celebrate or complain?

### Key Questions

- Jonah quotes God's own self-description as a complaint — what does it mean that divine mercy can be experienced as offensive?
- Why does Jonah care more about a plant than about a hundred and twenty thousand people — and where do you see that same disproportion in yourself?
- The book ends with an unanswered question. What is your answer?
- Is there someone in your life whose repentance you would resent rather than celebrate — and what does that reveal about your understanding of grace?
- How does Jonah's story challenge the idea that theological correctness and moral maturity are the same thing?

## Habakkuk — How Long, O LORD? The Prophet Who Argued with God (Habakkuk 1:1-2:4)

### Summary

Habakkuk is unlike any other prophetic book because the prophet does not speak to the people on God's behalf — he speaks to God on the people's behalf, and he is angry. "How long, LORD, must I call for help, but you do not listen? Or cry out to you, 'Violence!' but you do not save?" The prophet is looking at injustice in Judah — corruption, violence, legal oppression — and demanding to know why God allows it. This is not polite theological inquiry. This is a complaint, filed directly with the Almighty.

God answers, but the answer is worse than the silence. God is raising up the Babylonians — "that ruthless and impetuous people, who sweep across the whole earth to seize dwellings not their own." God describes the Babylonians in terrifying detail: their horses are swifter than leopards, fiercer than wolves at dusk, their cavalry gallops from afar, flying like eagles swooping to devour. They come bent on violence, gather prisoners like sand. They mock kings and scoff at rulers. God's answer to injustice in Judah is... a worse injustice. A more violent, more powerful empire that will crush Judah along with everything else.

Habakkuk is appalled. "Your eyes are too pure to look on evil; you cannot tolerate wrongdoing. Why then do you tolerate the treacherous? Why are you silent while the wicked swallow up those more righteous than themselves?" The prophet has moved from complaining about Judah's injustice to complaining about God's solution to Judah's injustice. He stations himself on the rampart and waits for God's response.

The response comes, and it is the sentence that would change the world: "The righteous shall live by faith." The full context is: "See, the enemy is puffed up; his desires are not upright — but the righteous person will live by his faithfulness." The answer to the unanswerable question of why God permits evil is not an explanation but an instruction: trust. Live by faith. Not because the situation makes sense, but because God can be trusted even when God's methods are incomprehensible.

### Significance

Habakkuk is the Bible's most sustained wrestling match between a human being and God over the problem of evil, and it is remarkable for what it does not provide: a satisfying philosophical answer. God does not explain why evil exists or why the response to one evil is a greater evil. Instead, God offers a relational answer: trust me. "The righteous shall live by faith" is not a theological formula — it is a survival strategy for people who cannot understand what God is doing but refuse to stop trusting.

This verse (2:4b) became one of the most influential sentences in the history of religion. Paul quotes it in Romans 1:17 and Galatians 3:11 as the foundation of his theology of justification by faith. Martin Luther's reading of Paul's reading of Habakkuk became the spark that ignited the Protestant Reformation. A verse spoken to a bewildered prophet in seventh-century Judah became the theological engine that reshaped Western Christianity. The chain of influence is extraordinary: Habakkuk to Paul to Luther to the modern world.

### Relational Note

What makes Habakkuk unique among the prophets is that he does not accept God's answer without argument. He complains, receives an answer, and then complains about the answer. This is not presented as unfaithful — it is presented as the prophet's legitimate function. Habakkuk models a relationship with God that includes protest, objection, and continued engagement even when the answers are unsatisfying.

For anyone who has ever been told to "just trust God" in the face of inexplicable suffering, Habakkuk offers both validation and challenge. The validation: your questions are legitimate, and even prophets ask them. The challenge: at some point, you must choose between demanding an explanation you may never receive and trusting a God whose methods you may never understand. Habakkuk chooses trust — but he chooses it with his eyes open, from the watchtower, still looking for answers.

### Conceptual Note

The theological problem Habakkuk raises — how can a just God use an unjust instrument? — has never been satisfactorily resolved by any philosopher or theologian. It is a version of the problem of evil that adds a specifically biblical dimension: God is not merely allowing evil but actively deploying it. The Babylonians are not acting on their own; God says "I am raising up" the Babylonians. This makes God complicit in the violence, and Habakkuk knows it, which is why his second complaint is even more anguished than his first.

"The righteous shall live by faith" (Hebrew: emunah, faithfulness/steadfastness) does not resolve the philosophical problem. It redirects it. The question shifts from "why does God allow evil?" to "how do I live in a world where God allows evil?" The answer is not an intellectual framework but a posture: faithfulness, steadfastness, continued trust in the character of God even when the actions of God are incomprehensible. This is faith not as belief but as endurance.

### Climate Note

Habakkuk almost certainly prophesied in the late seventh century BCE, between the fall of Assyria and the rise of Babylon. The "violence" and "injustice" he observes in Judah likely reflect the reign of Jehoiakim (609-598 BCE), who was condemned by Jeremiah for forced labor, injustice, and the murder of innocents. The Babylonian threat was not hypothetical — Nebuchadnezzar's armies would besiege Jerusalem in 597 and destroy it in 586 BCE.

The prophet's act of stationing himself on the "rampart" (matsor) to await God's answer evokes the physical reality of walled cities in the late Iron Age. The rampart was the top of the city wall, a place for watchmen. Habakkuk positions himself as a watchman — someone whose job is to look outward for threats and report what he sees. The metaphor suggests that the prophet's role is to keep watching, keep asking, even when the view from the wall is nothing but approaching armies.

### Modern Parallel

A doctor in a war zone prays every night for God to stop the bombing. The bombing continues. She prays for medical supplies. None come. She prays for the children in her care to survive. Some do. Some don't. After months, she writes in her journal: "I have asked God a thousand times why. The only answer I have received is more questions. I am told to trust. I do not understand what I am trusting. I trust anyway, because the alternative is to stop caring, and I am not ready to do that."

This is Habakkuk's situation. He does not receive an answer to the problem of evil. He receives an instruction: live by faith. The instruction does not make the situation comprehensible. It makes the situation survivable. Faith, in Habakkuk, is not the absence of doubt — it is the decision to keep going despite doubt. It is not a feeling — it is a practice. The righteous shall live by emunah: by steadfastness, by endurance, by the stubborn refusal to let go of a God whose actions make no sense.

The influence of this single verse is staggering. Paul read Habakkuk and built a theology of grace. Luther read Paul reading Habakkuk and started a revolution. But the original context is not about doctrine — it is about survival. A bewildered man on a city wall, watching the most dangerous army in the world approach, and choosing to trust the God who sent it. That is the kind of faith that changes history.

### Key Questions

- Is it faithful to argue with God, or does genuine faith require accepting divine decisions without protest?
- God's answer to injustice in Judah is a more powerful injustice (Babylon) — how do you process the idea that God uses evil instruments?
- 'The righteous shall live by faith' — is this an answer to the problem of evil, or a redirection away from it?
- How does Habakkuk's complaint change when you read it alongside the complaints of Job, Jeremiah, and the lament psalms?
- Where in your life are you stationed on the rampart, watching for an answer that has not yet come?
